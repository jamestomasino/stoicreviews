About Stoicism

Stoicism is a philosophy for everyday living. It has its origins in ancient Athens where it was one of the four principal schools of philosophy. With peers like Plato and Aristotle, a man named Zeno of Citium put together a mashup of Greek thought fit for a king (or emperor). Unlike philosophy as you may understand it today, in the ancient world it was a practical science.

The goal of stoicism is to be healthy, happy, and prosperous: a state summed up in the greek word Eudaimonia. Of course the stoic way of defining health, happiness, and prosperity are all a bit unique. It's best to think of eudaimonia as synonym of Tranquility.

The path toward tranquility is found in four characteristics: wisdom, courage, self-control, and justice. These four together are summed up in the Greek word Arete. The Greeks have a word for everything, don't they?

Basic Tenents

Physics - The stoics understood our universe to be made up of two key parts: the physical universe, and fortune. The physical world requires study, understanding, and respect. Fortune is harder to pin down. Fate, universal reason, God; many ideas could fit the intention. The physical world changes and grows. We change with it. The spirit of that change is in everything, including us. It naturally drives the world forward and if we pay attention, we can align ourselves to that path Fortune sets. Learn more about stoic physics.

Ethics - What is good? What is just? What is the purpose of our lives? The stoics believed we were made to serve each other. Society and its service is our highest calling. Put your efforts toward the betterment of all of humankind and live a good life. This was one of the major appeals to the Romans. Learn more about stoic ethics.

Logic - The type of logical thinking used by Zeno, Cato, Seneca and others is now known as propositional logic as a way to distinguish it from the logic of Aristotle. In short, it's the logic of statements, their deduction, and so on. In practice today, logic can be thought of as applying your reasoning to the Physical and Ethical questions. A good stoic applies this reason carefully and intentionally to everything. Learn more about stoic logic.

Famous Stoics

Seneca (4 BC - AD 65) - Seneca was a philosopher, playwright, statesman, and advisor to Emperor Nero. He was wealthy and famous despite a few exiles. In the end, he was caught up in a conspiracy he was likely not a part of, but forced to commit suicide nontheless. His importance to stoicism is primarily from his letters to his friend, Lucilius Junior. These letters are captured in the book, Letters from a Stoic.

{% picture sidepic seneca.jpg %}

Epictetus (AD 55 - 135) - The foundation of all philosophy is self-knowledge, or so the claim by Epictetus goes. He was born a slave, served as secretary to Emperor Nero, and was freed following Nero's death. After that time he found a following as a philosopher. Although we have none of his own writings, his lecture notes—captured by his student—later became his seminal work, Discourses. A shorter volume, Enchiridion—"Handbook"—which is somewhat like a CliffsNotes version of Discourses.

{% picture sidepic epictetus.jpg %}

Marcus Aurelius (AD 121 - 180) - The last of the five great emperors of Rome, Marcus spent most of his life at war abroad and facing instability at home. He was betrayed by his best friend, may have had an unfaithful wife, saw the deaths of several children, and overall just had a pretty rough go of things. Still, he was the most powerful man on the planet for a time, and folks generally thought he did a good job of it. He owes much of his ability to roll with the punches to his study of Epictetus' works. Marcus kept notes to himself throughout his life. One such journal kept during a campaigning decade formed the book Meditations. Of all the stoic writings, Meditations is perhaps the most well known and studied by lay people, presidents, CEOs, explorers, authors, etc.
